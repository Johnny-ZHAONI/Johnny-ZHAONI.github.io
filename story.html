Synopsis

Rickshaw Boy (Camel Xiangzi) follows a hardworking rickshaw puller in Republican‑era Beijing whose dream is to own his own modern rickshaw. After repeated setbacks—conscription and loss of his cart, police extortion, a mismatched marriage with Huniu, illness and poverty—Xiangzi’s self‑reliant idealism erodes. The death of Xiaofuzi, a neighbor burdened by family tragedy, becomes the breaking point. The novel charts Xiangzi’s slide from aspiration and dignity to disillusion and self‑destruction.

At a Glance

  • Author: Lao She
  • Form: Realist novel
  • First serialization: 1936–1937
  • First book edition: 1939
  • Setting: Beijing (Republican period)

Human Dignity

Close attention to the lives of urban laborers foregrounds empathy and a critique of social injustice.

Environment & Decline

Multiple blows show how hostile conditions can hollow out self‑help ideals and personal agency.

Realism & Irony

Plainspoken narration and vivid Beijing idiom create psychological depth while sustaining satire.

Publication & Editions

  • 1936–1937: Serialized in magazines; 1939: first book edition by Renjian Book Company.
  • 1955: A revised edition removed passages; a 1983 collected works version substantially restored deletions.
